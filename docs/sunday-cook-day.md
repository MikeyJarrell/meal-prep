# Sunday cook day

last-reviewed: 2026-07-05

The weekly rhythm: pick 2 lunches and 2 or 3 dinners in the app, add burritos every other week, generate the list, shop, then cook everything in one afternoon. Two good cooks working in parallel finish a normal week (burritos, 2 lunches, 2 braised or simmered dinners, one no-cook dinner prep) in about 3 to 4 hours including cleanup. The recipes carry their own steps; this page is about sequencing them so the afternoon doesn't sprawl.

## Order of operations

1. **Braises in first, before anything else.** Anything with an unattended oven phase — carnitas, ragù, chile verde — gets assembled and into the oven at the start. They all braise at 300°F, so they share the oven happily. Their 2.5 to 3 hours run underneath everything below.

2. **Marinades second.** Shawarma, tikka, bulgogi, char siu, lemongrass pork: mix the marinade and get the meat soaking. Time in the marinade is free flavor while you do other things.

3. **Rice and grains on a loop.** The rice pot (or cooker) cycles all afternoon: one batch per recipe that needs it, spread on a sheet pan to cool fast, then boxed. Fried-rice rice goes into the fridge uncovered on purpose — it should dry out.

4. **One grill session, everything through it.** Light the grill once and run every grilled protein over it back to back: shawarma thighs, tikka thighs, lemongrass pork. Same fire, ten-minute turnarounds.

5. **The small-jar assembly line.** All the sauces, dressings, and quick pickles in one stretch: nuoc cham, peanut dressing, tahini sauce, gochujang sauce, burger sauce, pickled onions, cucumbers, cabbage, carrots. This is satisfying, fast, and the single biggest reason weekday food tastes finished. Small jars or sauce cups; sauce touching food before Thursday is how salads die.

6. **Soups and simmered things** (gumbo, bean soup, curries, tinga, chana) run on the stovetop in parallel with the oven braises. The gumbo roux wants 30 undivided minutes of stirring — assign it to whoever isn't managing the grill.

7. **Burrito assembly last**, once its components have cooled — rolling warm fillings into tortillas is the classic sog mistake. See the recipe for the cooling logic.

## Cooling, packing, and the day-5 problem

Cool fast, then lid: pots go into a sink of cold water for 15 minutes, or food gets spread on sheet pans; containers go into the fridge uncovered until they stop steaming, then get lids. Lidding hot food steams everything soggy and warms the fridge.

Lunches pack as five or six individual containers per recipe — grain down, protein on, wet and crunchy things in corners or sauce cups. Dinners pack family-style in big containers; portions happen at reheat.

Cooked food is honestly at its best through day 4, so run the fridge-freezer split on Sunday: anything being eaten Thursday or Friday from a freezer-friendly batch (soups, braises, gumbo, ragù, tinga) gets frozen Sunday night and moved to the fridge Wednesday. It costs nothing and Friday tastes like Monday. Salad-style lunches (lentil, noodle, orzo, peanut chicken) are built to run the full five days in the fridge — that's why they're in the library.

Fish is not in the library at all: nothing about day-4 reheated fish deserves you.

## The weekly pick, roughly balanced

A good default week: one cold lunch and one microwave lunch; one braise-ahead dinner and one fast-finish dinner (pizza, smash burgers, bibimbap, fried rice), plus a third if the week needs it. That balances the Sunday load — braises are passive, fast-finish dinners cost Sunday almost nothing — and it means weeknights alternate between "microwave" and "ten fun minutes at the flat top."
