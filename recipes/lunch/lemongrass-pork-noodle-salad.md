A bún-style bowl: rice noodles, herbs, cucumber, pickled carrot, and thin-sliced lemongrass pork with real char, with nuoc cham poured over at lunch. This is the cold-lunch flagship — nothing in the container wants a microwave, the flavors get louder as the week goes on, and it eats light without being small.

## Ingredients

- 2.5 lb | pork shoulder | meat | from the Costco two-pack; freeze the rest for a braise week
- 3 stalks | lemongrass | other-store | or about 45 g of lemongrass paste from the tube
- 2 | shallots | produce
- 8 cloves | garlic | produce | 6 for the marinade, 2 for the nuoc cham
- 450 g | rice noodles | other-store | the vermicelli-width ones
- 6 | persian cucumbers | produce
- 3 | carrots | produce
- 1 head | butter lettuce | produce
- 1 bunch | mint | produce
- 1 bunch | cilantro | produce
- 5 | limes | produce
- 1 | jalapeños | produce
- 75 g | roasted peanuts | pantry

### Pantry staples

- fish sauce
- brown sugar
- neutral oil
- rice vinegar, for the pickled carrot
- black pepper

## Sunday

1. Put the pork in the freezer first thing, for 30 to 45 minutes, until it is stiff at the edges but not frozen solid — firm meat slices far thinner than soft meat, and thin slices are what char fast. Then look at the direction the muscle fibers run and slice across them (this is slicing across the grain, which makes each bite tender), as thin as you can manage, aiming for about a quarter inch. This is the one knife job of the day that rewards patience.
2. Make the marinade while the pork firms up — the blender turns the worst knife work of the day into a 30-second job: trim the lemongrass by cutting off the root end and the tough green top so only the pale bottom third of each stalk remains, peel away the tough papery outer layers until you reach the tender pale core, and slice the cores into thin rounds so the blades can catch them (or squeeze the paste from the tube and skip the stalk prep). Blend the lemongrass, the shallots halved, {6} peeled garlic cloves, {54 g} fish sauce, {42 g} brown sugar, {27 g} neutral oil, and lots of black pepper into a rough paste, scraping down the jar once or twice. Pour it over the sliced pork in a big bowl and massage it in with your hands until every slice is coated, then let it sit at least 30 minutes while you do everything else — marinating just means letting the meat soak in its seasoning.
3. Pickle the carrots: peel them and cut them into thin matchsticks (this cut is called julienne), then pile them into a jar or container with a lid. In a small saucepan, heat {180 g} rice vinegar, {90 g} water, {40 g} sugar, and {8 g} salt over medium heat, stirring, until the sugar dissolves, about 2 minutes — steaming is enough, no boil needed. Pour the hot brine over the carrots, put the lid on, and refrigerate. They pickle themselves from here.
4. Make the nuoc cham (the sauce that gets poured over everything at lunch): in a bowl, stir together {108 g} fish sauce, the juice of {4} limes, {50 g} sugar, {180 g} water, {2} grated garlic cloves, and the jalapeño minced fine (scrape out the seeds first for less heat), until the sugar fully dissolves. Taste it — it should snap: salty, sour, and sweet all at once, louder than seems right, because it has to season a whole bowl of plain noodles. Portion it into six small jars or sauce cups.
5. Cook the noodles: bring a big pot of salted water to a rolling boil over high heat, add the noodles, and cook for the time on the package — thin rice noodles go fast, so start checking early; they are done when tender with no chalky core. Drain them in a colander and immediately rinse under cold running water, tossing with your hands, until they are fully cool — the cold rinse stops the cooking and washes off surface starch. Shake them dry and toss with a small splash of neutral oil so they stay loose instead of fusing into a brick.
6. Cook the pork over the hottest heat you have — a grill or a flat-top. Lay the slices in a single layer with space between them and cook about 2 minutes a side, working in batches. Done right, the edges turn deep brown with a few nearly-black spots and the kitchen smells sweet and smoky — that is the brown sugar in the marinade caramelizing, and that char is the whole point. Do not crowd the pan: slices touching each other trap steam and turn gray instead of browning. Move each finished batch to a plate before starting the next.
7. Pack six containers: noodles across the bottom, then a big handful of torn lettuce, the cucumbers cut lengthwise into spears, a tangle of pickled carrot, the pork, and the mint and cilantro leaves picked off their stems and scattered dry on top. Peanuts go in a corner or a twist of foil so they stay crunchy, and the sauce jar rides on the side. Leave the lids off until nothing feels warm, about 20 minutes, then lid and refrigerate.

## Weeknight

- No reheating — this one is built to eat cold. Pour the nuoc cham over everything, toss with a fork until the noodles are coated, and scatter the peanuts on top.

## Notes

- Lemongrass paste is a fine weekday-brain substitute; the fresh stalks are noticeably better if the Asian market run happens.
- Kendra version: this is the light one already — extra lettuce and herbs, and the portion still eats big.
- Day 5 check: noodles and pickles are fine Friday; keep the herbs dry when packing and they make it too.
