Crisped harissa chickpeas and a sheet pan of deeply roasted vegetables over lemony quinoa, with feta and a garlicky yogurt sauce. One of the two vegetarian lunches, and the one that converts vegetable skeptics — the trick is roasting hotter and longer than feels polite, so everything caramelizes instead of steaming.

## Ingredients

- 3 cans | chickpeas | pantry | from the Costco 8-pack
- 115 g | harissa | other-store | one small jar; brands run mild to scary, taste yours first
- 3 | zucchini | produce
- 2 | bell peppers | produce
- 2 | red onions | produce
- 300 g | cherry tomatoes | produce
- 340 g | quinoa | pantry | measured dry; the big Costco bag
- 3 | lemons | produce
- 1 bunch | flat-leaf parsley | produce
- 170 g | feta | dairy-eggs
- 250 g | plain greek yogurt | dairy-eggs
- 2 cloves | garlic | produce

### Pantry staples

- olive oil
- kosher salt and black pepper
- ground cumin and smoked paprika
- honey

## Sunday

1. Heat the oven to 450°F with both sheet pans inside while it heats — putting the vegetables on already-hot metal makes them start browning the moment they land, instead of slowly steaming in their own juices on a cold pan. Let the oven come fully up to temperature while you do the next two steps.
2. Get the chickpeas dry: drain them in a colander, spread them on a clean kitchen towel, and rub and pat them hard until they are truly dry — dry chickpeas crisp in the oven, wet ones steam. A few papery skins will rub loose; discard them or leave them, either is fine. Tip the chickpeas into a big bowl and toss with a glug of olive oil, the harissa (taste a dab first — brands run mild to scary, so hold some back if yours bites), a big pinch of cumin, and a big pinch of salt.
3. Cut the vegetables: halve the zucchini lengthwise and slice into half-moons about as thick as your thumb; halve the peppers, pull out the stems and seeds, and cut into wide strips; peel the red onions and cut into wedges. The cherry tomatoes stay whole. Toss everything in a big bowl with a glug of olive oil, a couple of big pinches of salt, and a big pinch of smoked paprika.
4. Pull the hot pans out with oven mitts — they are as hot as the oven — and spread the chickpeas on one and the vegetables in a single layer on the other; they should sizzle when they hit the metal. Roast about 25 minutes, pulling the pans out once around the halfway mark to shake the chickpeas and turn the vegetables with a spatula. Done looks like this: the chickpeas are browned and rattle drily when you shake the pan, and the vegetables have real browned, caramelized edges with the onion tips going dark. Pale and wet means not done — give them another 5 minutes and err on the side of more color.
5. Meanwhile, cook the quinoa: rinse {340 g} quinoa in a fine strainer under cold water for about 30 seconds (this washes off its naturally bitter coating). Put it in a lidded saucepan with {646 g} water and a pinch of salt, bring to a boil over high heat, then cover, turn the heat to low, and cook 15 minutes, until the water is absorbed and each grain has popped a tiny curled tail. Turn off the heat, leave it covered 5 minutes, then fluff with a fork. While it is still warm, zest 2 of the lemons (grate just the yellow outer layer of peel on a fine grater, stopping before the bitter white layer underneath) and stir in the zest, the juice of those 2 lemons, a glug of olive oil, and a pinch of salt.
6. Yogurt sauce: in a small bowl, stir the yogurt with the garlic grated fine, the juice of the last lemon, a pinch of salt, and a thin thread of honey. Divide into six sauce cups.
7. Pack six containers: quinoa across the bottom, roasted vegetables and chickpeas over it, crumbled feta and roughly chopped parsley in a corner, sauce cup on the side. Leave the lids off until the food stops steaming, about 20 minutes, then lid and refrigerate.

## Weeknight

- Take the sauce cup out and microwave 2 minutes, or let the container sit out a few minutes and eat it at room temperature — both are legitimate. Spoon the yogurt sauce over just before eating.

## Notes

- The chickpeas de-crisp in the fridge no matter what you do; they trade crunch for chew and are still good. Accept it.
- Kendra version: as written. Mikey version: add a grilled thigh from the shawarma batch or a scoop of leftover tinga — the bowls take passengers happily.
- Day 5 check: everything holds; the tomatoes get jammier, which is a feature.
