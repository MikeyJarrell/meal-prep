Pork shoulder braised in a salsa of tomatillos, poblanos, and jalapeños that you char under the broiler or on the grill until blackened in spots — the char is the smoke, the tomatillos are the acid, and the pork is the excuse. A braise in a bright green sauce reheats all week without losing a step, and the leftover salsa logic works overtime: spoon it on eggs, tacos, or the rice bowls of lesser lunches.

## Ingredients

- 3.5 lb | pork shoulder | meat | the second half of the two-pack, or the freezer twin from bún week
- 2 lb | tomatillos | other-store | husked and rinsed of their stickiness
- 3 | poblano peppers | other-store
- 2 | jalapeños | produce
- 2 | yellow onions | produce
- 8 cloves | garlic | produce
- 1 bunch | cilantro | produce | stems into the blender, leaves for serving
- 480 g | chicken stock | pantry
- 370 g | jasmine rice | pantry | measured dry
- 2 cans | black beans | pantry
- 12 | corn tortillas | pantry
- 2 | limes | produce

### Pantry staples

- neutral oil
- kosher salt and black pepper
- ground cumin
- dried oregano (Mexican if you have it)

## Sunday

1. Husk the tomatillos: peel off the papery outer wrappers, then rinse the fruit under warm water, rubbing with your fingers until the sticky film is gone — they should feel like smooth, slightly waxy green tomatoes. Cut the onions in half through the root and peel them.
2. Set the broiler to high (or use a hot grill). Spread the tomatillos, whole poblanos, whole jalapeños, and onion halves on a sheet pan and broil close to the heat for 10 to 12 minutes, turning everything once with tongs, until blackened in spots. It will look burnt in places, and that is correct — the char is where the smoky flavor comes from.
3. Move the poblanos to a bowl, cover it with a plate, and leave them 10 minutes — the trapped steam loosens the skins. Rub and peel off the worst of the blackened skin with your fingers (do not rinse them under water; that washes away flavor), then pull out the stems and seed clusters. Leave the tomatillos and jalapeños as they are; if you want a milder verde, split the jalapeños and scrape out the seeds.
4. Put the charred vegetables and any juices from the sheet pan into a blender with the peeled garlic, the cilantro stems, and {5 g} salt. Pulse in short bursts into a coarse salsa — chunky, not smooth. Set it aside and heat the oven to 300°F.
5. Cut the pork into 2-inch chunks and salt them all over. Set the dutch oven over medium-high heat with enough neutral oil to film the bottom and heat until the oil shimmers. Brown the pork in 2 or 3 batches: single layer, space between the pieces, and leave each piece alone 2 to 3 minutes per side until it has a deep brown crust. Batches matter — a crowded pot traps steam and the meat turns gray instead of brown. Set each batch aside on a plate as it finishes.
6. Return all the pork and its juices to the pot. Pour in the salsa and the stock, add a big pinch each of cumin and oregano, and scrape the bottom of the pot with a wooden spoon so the browned bits dissolve into the sauce. Bring it to a simmer on the stove.
7. Set the lid on slightly cracked and put the pot in the 300°F oven for about 2.5 hours. This is a braise: cooking low and slow in liquid until the tough parts of the meat melt and it gives up completely. It is done when a chunk falls apart under gentle pressure from a spoon; if it resists, give it 30 more minutes. Then skim (spoon the clear fat off the surface — a little left behind is fine), shred the pork coarsely into the sauce with two forks, taste for salt, and squeeze in the juice of a lime off the heat — adding the lime off the heat is what keeps the sauce bright green.
8. While the pork braises, cook the rice: put {370 g} jasmine rice in a strainer and rinse under cold water for about 30 seconds. Rice cooker: rice plus {465 g} water, press start. Pot: rice and {465 g} water in a lidded pot, bring to a boil over high heat, then cover, turn the heat to its lowest setting, and cook 15 minutes; turn off the heat and leave it covered 10 more minutes without lifting the lid. Warm the beans in a small saucepan over medium-low heat with a pinch of salt, about 5 minutes, until steaming.
9. Pack dinner containers: rice and beans on one side, verde on the other. Leave the lids off until the food stops steaming, about 20 minutes, then lid and refrigerate. Freeze a third of the verde if it features only once this week.

## Weeknight

- Microwave the verde, rice, and beans 3 to 4 minutes until steaming hot, stirring the verde once halfway through.
- While they heat, warm the tortillas on the flat top 20 seconds a side until soft and puffed in spots, and stack them in a clean towel. Cilantro leaves and a squeeze of lime over everything at the table.

## Notes

- Tomatillos and poblanos are the grocery-store stop of the week; the rest is Costco.
- Kendra version: verde over shredded cabbage with beans, skipping rice — it eats like a warm salad, in a good way.
- Day 5 check: braise in sauce, no drama. The green stays green if you added the lime at the end, off heat.
