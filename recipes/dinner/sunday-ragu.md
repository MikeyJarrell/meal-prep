Chuck roast and Italian sausage braised into a proper meat sauce — wine, tomato, the soffritto trinity, three lazy hours in the oven. Ragù is the single best argument for cooking on Sunday and eating on Wednesday: it needs time twice, once in the oven and once in the fridge. Pasta boils fresh in the ten minutes the sauce takes to reheat, so weeknight-you does almost nothing and eats like a Sunday.

## Ingredients

- 2.5 lb | beef chuck roast | meat | cut into 2-inch chunks
- 1 lb | mild italian sausage | meat | out of the casings
- 2 | yellow onions | produce
- 3 | carrots | produce
- 3 ribs | celery | produce
- 6 cloves | garlic | produce
- 50 g | tomato paste | pantry
- 1 can | whole peeled tomatoes (800 g can) | pantry | crushed by hand
- 1 can | crushed tomatoes (800 g can) | pantry
- 480 g | chicken stock | pantry
- 1.5 lb | rigatoni | pantry | the Costco Garofalo 6-pack earns its shelf space
- 85 g | parmigiano reggiano | dairy-eggs | plus the rind into the pot

### Pantry staples

- olive oil
- kosher salt and black pepper
- {350 g} dry red wine — anything you would drink
- bay leaves
- chili flakes

## Sunday

1. Heat the oven to 300°F. While it heats, cut the chuck into 2-inch chunks if it is not already and salt them all over. Finely dice the onions, carrots, and celery into pieces the size of a pea or smaller, and mince the garlic.
2. Set the dutch oven over medium-high heat with a glug of olive oil and heat until the oil shimmers. Brown the chuck hard in 2 or 3 batches: single layer, space between the pieces, and leave each piece alone 2 to 3 minutes per side until every face has a deep brown crust. Batches matter — a crowded pot traps steam and the meat turns gray instead of brown, and that brown crust is flavor. Set the browned beef aside on a plate.
3. Brown the sausage in the same pot: drop it in by big pinches, break it into crumbles with the spoon, and cook about 5 minutes until browned in spots. Set it aside with the beef. Do not clean the pot — the dark crust stuck to the bottom is the start of the sauce.
4. Make the soffritto (the Italian flavor base: onion, carrot, and celery cooked slowly until soft and sweet): turn the heat down to medium, add the diced vegetables and a pinch of salt, and cook 10 minutes, stirring often, until soft and translucent but not browned. Add the garlic and the tomato paste and stir 2 minutes until the paste darkens from bright red to brick.
5. Deglaze with the wine: pour it in and scrape the bottom of the pot with a wooden spoon until the browned bits let go and dissolve into the liquid. Let it bubble 4 to 5 minutes until reduced by half — reducing just means boiling liquid away until about half remains, which concentrates the flavor.
6. Crush the whole peeled tomatoes by hand straight into the pot — squeeze each one low over the surface so it does not spray — then add the can of crushed tomatoes, the stock, 2 bay leaves, a pinch of chili flakes, the parmesan rind, and all the meat with its juices. Bring it to a simmer on the stove.
7. Set the lid on slightly cracked and put the pot in the 300°F oven for about 3 hours. This is a braise: cooking low and slow in liquid until the tough connective tissue in the chuck melts. There is nothing to do while it goes — cook the rest of Sunday around it. It is done when a chunk breaks apart under a spoon with no resistance; if it pushes back, give it 30 more minutes.
8. Fish out the bay leaves and the parmesan rind. Shred the beef coarsely with two forks right in the pot, skim the obvious fat (spoon the clear pools off the surface — a little left behind is flavor), and taste for salt.
9. Cool the pot fast: set it in a sink with a few inches of cold water and stir every few minutes until it is warm rather than hot, 20 to 30 minutes. Divide into three weeknight-size containers and refrigerate; freeze one if ragù features only once this week.

## Weeknight

- Put a big pot of water on to boil over high heat and salt it aggressively — it should taste like seawater. Meanwhile warm the night's sauce in a wide pan over low heat, stirring now and then (or microwave it warm first, then move it to the pan).
- Boil the night's portion of rigatoni 1 minute short of the box time, and save a ladleful of the cloudy cooking water before you drain. Tip the drained pasta into the sauce pan with a splash of that pasta water and toss over medium heat for the last minute, until the sauce clings to the pasta instead of pooling under it. Parmesan grated over without restraint.

## Notes

- The fresh-boiled pasta is non-negotiable machinery of this plan: sauce keeps beautifully, cooked pasta does not.
- Kendra version: half portion of pasta, same ladle of ragù, arugula salad from the lunch stock alongside.
- Day 5 check: ragù peaks days 2 through 4 and is still excellent day 5. Also freezes for a month, which makes it the emergency-dinner reserve.
