All the banchan — sesame spinach, sweet carrots, pickled cucumber, kimchi — prepped Sunday, so bibimbap night is a 15-minute flat-top event: bulgogi seared in ninety-second batches, an egg fried in the beef fat, gochujang sauce over the top. Marinating the beef all week is not a storage compromise; bulgogi wants the soak. If a scoop of rice gets pressed onto the hot griddle until it crackles first, no one will call the health department.

## Ingredients

- 2 lb | shaved beef top round (shabu-shabu) | meat | pre-shaved at Costco; flank steak sliced thin is the fallback
- 8 cloves | garlic | produce
- 55 g | fresh ginger | produce
- 1 | apples | produce | blended into the marinade, standing in for Korean pear
- 555 g | jasmine rice | pantry | measured dry; short-grain if you keep it
- 285 g | baby spinach | produce
- 4 | carrots | produce
- 4 | persian cucumbers | produce
- 450 g | kimchi | dairy-eggs | the Jongga jar at Costco
- 80 g | gochujang | pantry
- 60 g | plain greek yogurt | dairy-eggs | for the sauce; it softens the heat
- 6 | large eggs | dairy-eggs
- 1 bunch | green onions | produce

### Pantry staples

- soy sauce
- brown sugar
- toasted sesame oil
- rice vinegar and sugar, for the pickles
- sesame seeds
- neutral oil
- honey

## Sunday

1. Marinade first, so the beef gets the longest soak — the blender does all the grating: peel the garlic and set 1 clove aside for the spinach in step 3. Cut the apple into rough chunks around the core (skin on), and peel the ginger (scraping with the edge of a spoon takes the skin off easily) and cut it into coins. Blend the apple, ginger, the rest of the garlic, {108 g} soy, {42 g} brown sugar, {27 g} sesame oil, and a few grinds of black pepper until smooth, about 30 seconds. Pour it over the shaved beef in a big lidded container and toss with your hands until every ribbon is coated, then lid it and refrigerate. It sits until its night, and the flavor deepens daily — the long soak is the point, not a storage compromise. (Starting from flank instead: freeze it 30 minutes so it firms up, slice paper-thin across the grain, then marinate.)
2. Now the banchan — the little vegetable side dishes. There are three, and they stay separate the whole way: each one is cooked, seasoned, and boxed on its own, never mixed. The mixing happens in the bowl on the night you eat. One pan does all three, no washing between.
3. Sesame spinach: wilt the spinach in a wide pan over medium-high heat with a film of neutral oil, adding it a few big handfuls at a time as it collapses, 2 to 3 minutes total. Let it cool enough to handle, then get the water out: a few pulses in the salad spinner do most of the work, and one firm hand-squeeze over the sink finishes it. Toss with {14 g} sesame oil, {12 g} soy sauce, the clove from step 1 grated over the bowl, and a big pinch of sesame seeds. Box it.
4. Sweet carrots: cut the carrots into matchsticks — slice thin diagonal planks, stack the planks, slice the stack into skinny sticks (that move is all "julienne" means). Sauté in the same pan over medium-high heat with a pinch each of sugar and salt, about 2 minutes — just softened, still snappy. Box it.
5. Pickled cucumbers: a quick pickle is just a vinegar soak — no canning, no equipment, crunchy in an hour. Slice the cucumbers into thin coins and put them in a jar. In a small saucepan over medium heat, warm {120 g} rice vinegar, {60 g} water, {25 g} sugar, and {5 g} salt, stirring, until the sugar dissolves, about 2 minutes — steaming is enough, it does not need to boil. Pour it over the cucumbers, lid on, into the fridge.
6. Sauce: stir {80 g} gochujang, {60 g} plain greek yogurt, {14 g} sesame oil, {21 g} honey, and {15 g} rice vinegar together in a jar — the yogurt rounds the gochujang's heat into something creamier — then loosen with water, a small splash at a time, stirring between splashes, until it runs off a spoon in a ribbon instead of sitting in a blob. Lid it and refrigerate; it keeps all week.
7. Cook the rice: put {555 g} jasmine rice in a strainer and rinse under cold water for about 30 seconds. Rice cooker: rice plus {695 g} water, press start. Pot: rice and {695 g} water in a lidded pot, bring to a boil over high heat, then cover, turn the heat to its lowest setting, and cook 15 minutes; turn off the heat and leave it covered 10 more minutes without lifting the lid. Box the rice once it stops steaming, then line everything up on one fridge shelf like a little shop: marinating beef, three banchan boxes, sauce jar, pickle jar. (Kimchi is banchan number four; it arrives ready-made in its own jar and just needs to live nearby.)

## Weeknight

- Take the beef, banchan, sauce, and rice out of the fridge, then heat the flat top on its highest setting for a full 10 minutes — the fast sear only works on ripping-hot metal.
- In a hurry, the wok is the faster tool: its burner hits searing heat in 2 to 3 minutes instead of 10. Sear the drained beef in 2 or 3 single-layer batches, 30 to 45 seconds each with one toss, then fry the eggs in the wok after the beef. The rest of the night runs exactly the same.
- While it heats, drain the beef hard: tip it into a colander over the sink and press on it with your hands until liquid stops streaming off. The thin shave sheds marinade, and wet beef steams gray on the griddle instead of searing brown. Slice a few green onions thin while you wait.
- Sear the beef in batches: lay a single layer on the griddle with a little neutral oil, no pieces overlapping, and leave it alone about 30 seconds until the underside browns, then flip once and give it 15 more — 30 to 45 seconds total, and it is done when the edges darken and the marinade caramelizes. Scrape the batch to a cool corner and repeat. Never crowd it; a crowded griddle cools off and the beef stews.
- Fry the eggs alongside in a splash of neutral oil (top round renders less fat than flank, so the griddle needs the help): crack them on and leave them until the edges turn brown and lacy and the whites are set but the yolks still wobble when nudged, about 2 minutes. Do not flip them — a loose yolk is part of the sauce.
- Microwave the rice 2 to 3 minutes until steaming. For the crispy-bottom version, press a scoop flat onto the hot griddle with a spatula and leave it 2 minutes until it crackles and the underside turns golden and crunchy.
- Build the bowls: rice on the bottom, beef over one side, the three banchan and kimchi in wedges around it, egg on top, sauce drizzled over everything, sesame seeds and green onions to finish. Stir the whole bowl together before eating — bibimbap means mixed rice, and the mixing is the eating.

## Notes

- The fast sear only works on paper-thin beef; the Costco shabu-shabu pack wins the dish in the meat case. Hand-sliced flank runs a little thicker — give it the old 60-plus-30-second treatment.
- Kendra version: banchan-heavy bowl, half rice, one egg — bibimbap flexes lighter better than any dinner here.
- Day 5 check: banchan and marinating beef are Korean fridge cuisine working exactly as designed; Friday is prime.
