A proper dark-roux gumbo with chicken thighs and andouille — the roux goes to milk-chocolate-past-comfortable, the trinity hisses when it hits, and the pot gets better every single day it sits. Gumbo is arguably not even meant to be eaten on cooking day; making it Sunday and eating it Thursday is not meal prep, it is correct procedure with a permission slip.

## Ingredients

- 2 lb | boneless skinless chicken thighs | meat
- 1 lb | andouille sausage | meat | Costco often has Aidells; any good smoked sausage rescues a bad day
- 2 | yellow onions | produce
- 4 ribs | celery | produce
- 2 | bell peppers | produce
- 6 cloves | garlic | produce
- 1920 g | chicken stock | pantry | two quart boxes
- 340 g | frozen okra | other-store | optional but traditional; it thickens and it belongs
- 1 bunch | green onions | produce
- 465 g | jasmine rice | pantry | measured dry

### Pantry staples

- neutral oil and flour, for the roux ({160 g} oil, {90 g} flour)
- kosher salt and black pepper
- smoked paprika, cayenne, dried thyme, dried oregano, bay leaves
- hot sauce — the vinegary Louisiana kind

## Sunday

1. Prep everything before any heat goes on, because the roux will not let you stop to chop: dice the onions, celery, and bell peppers small and pile them into one bowl — that trio is the trinity, the onion-celery-pepper base that starts most Cajun cooking. Mince the garlic; measure the thyme, oregano, smoked paprika, and cayenne into a small bowl with the bay leaves; slice the andouille into coins; and open the stock so it is ready to pour.
2. Sear the andouille in the big heavy pot over medium-high heat with a small glug of oil, stirring now and then, until the coins brown at the edges, about 5 minutes; scoop them out to a bowl. Salt the chicken thighs and brown them lightly in the same pot, about 3 minutes per side — golden in spots, nowhere near cooked through, since they finish in the gumbo — and set them aside whole. Leave the browned bits stuck to the pot bottom; that is fond, pure flavor, and it dissolves into the gumbo later.
3. Now the roux, the whole show. A roux is flour cooked slowly in oil, and it is both what thickens the gumbo and where its deep toasted flavor comes from. Add {160 g} oil to the pot over medium heat, sprinkle in {90 g} flour, and stir until smooth. Then keep stirring — genuinely continuously, 25 to 35 minutes, scraping every corner of the pot bottom. The color tells you where you are: blond after a few minutes, peanut butter around the middle, then milk chocolate, and finally the color of an old penny — that is where you stop. It smells nutty, then toasty, then slightly alarming; that last one is right.
4. Two warnings while you stir. First, hot roux is what Cajun cooks call Cajun napalm — far hotter than boiling water, and it sticks to skin — so do not touch it, do not taste it, and keep anyone small away from the pot. Second, if black specks appear, the roux burned and the batch is bitter beyond rescue: dump it, wash the pot, and start the roux again. Medium-not-high heat and constant stirring are the insurance.
5. Tip the trinity straight into the hot roux and step back — it will roar and steam, and that is the plan, because the wet vegetables stop the roux from darkening further. Stir 5 minutes until the vegetables soften, then add the garlic, the spice bowl, and the bay leaves and stir 1 minute more until fragrant.
6. Whisk in the stock slowly: pour in a little, whisk until the roux absorbs it smoothly, then pour more — five or six additions in all, because dumping it in at once makes lumps that never leave. Return the chicken and andouille, bring to a simmer — small lazy bubbles, not a rolling boil — and cook with the lid ajar 45 minutes, stirring occasionally. Add the okra for the last 15. While it simmers, cook the rice: rinse {465 g} jasmine rice in a strainer, then either rice cooker with {580 g} water, or a lidded pot with {580 g} water — boil, cover, lowest heat 15 minutes, then 10 minutes off the heat with the lid on.
7. Pull the thighs out, shred them with two forks, and stir the meat back in. Season only now — the andouille and 45 minutes of reduction concentrate salt, so taste first, then add salt, black pepper, and hot sauce until it pops. Fish out the bay leaves. Cool the pot fast in a sink bath — set the pot in a few inches of cold water in the sink and stir occasionally — then portion into containers and freeze half for the back of the week.

## Weeknight

- Microwave the gumbo 3 to 4 minutes, stirring once halfway so the edges do not boil while the middle stays cold. Microwave the rice separately with a splash of water and a lid resting loosely on top, about 2 minutes — the steam wakes it back up.
- Slice a green onion or two thin. Rice in the bowl first, gumbo ladled over, green onions and more hot sauce on top.

## Notes

- The roux is the whole show — podcast, drink, stir. Do not answer the door.
- Kendra version: more broth, less rice; gumbo obliges.
- Day 5 check: the reference case for this repo's thesis. Day 5 gumbo beats day 1 gumbo in a blind taste, every time.
