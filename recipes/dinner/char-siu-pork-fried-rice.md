Pork shoulder in a hoisin-honey char siu glaze, roasted Sunday, and rice cooked Sunday specifically so it can dry out — because fried rice made from fresh rice is porridge, and fried rice made from three-day-old rice is the reason wok burners exist. This dinner is meal prep arguing from strength: both components are actively better for having waited.

## Ingredients

- 2 lb | pork shoulder | meat | a chunk off the Costco two-pack, cut into two long strips
- 160 g | hoisin sauce | other-store | Lee Kum Kee if choosing
- 555 g | jasmine rice | pantry | measured dry; cooked Sunday, deliberately staled in the fridge
- 4 | large eggs | dairy-eggs
- 200 g | frozen peas | frozen
- 2 | carrots | produce
- 1 bunch | green onions | produce
- 4 cloves | garlic | produce
- 30 g | fresh ginger | produce

### Pantry staples

- soy sauce
- honey
- toasted sesame oil
- five-spice powder
- neutral oil with a high smoke point
- white pepper if you have it, black if you don't

## Sunday

1. Marinade — one blender jar, no grating: peel the garlic and ginger (the edge of a spoon scrapes ginger skin off easily). Blend 2 cloves and half the ginger, cut into coins, with {160 g} hoisin, {63 g} honey, {54 g} soy, {14 g} sesame oil, and {2.5 g} five-spice until smooth. Before any pork touches it, spoon a few spoonfuls into a small container and refrigerate — that is the basting reserve for step 2, kept clean of raw meat. Coat the pork strips in the rest, turning until covered on all sides. Mince the remaining 2 cloves and the rest of the ginger, box them, and refrigerate — they are for the wok on fried-rice night.
2. Roast: set a wire rack over a foil-lined sheet pan, lay the pork strips on it, and roast at 375°F for about 90 minutes. In the back half, baste twice — basting just means spooning or brushing the reserved marinade over the meat so it builds a lacquered coat — at roughly the 50 and 70 minute marks. The pork is done when it looks burnished mahogany and a thermometer in the thickest part reads 190°F; that is far past the usual pork number on purpose, because shoulder at 190°F slices tender instead of rubbery. Finish under the broiler 2 minutes, watching the whole time, until the edges char and gloss. Cool until barely warm, slice into half-moons, box.
3. Cook the rice while the pork roasts: rinse {555 g} jasmine rice in a strainer under cold water about 30 seconds. Rice cooker: rice plus {695 g} water, press start. Pot: rice and {695 g} water, boil over high heat, then cover, drop to the lowest heat 15 minutes, and rest 10 more off the heat with the lid on. Then dry it out on purpose: spread the hot rice on a sheet pan so it cools fast, refrigerate the pan uncovered for an hour so the surface moisture evaporates, and box it. Dry rice is the entire fried-rice trick and Sunday is when it gets made — fresh rice steams itself into porridge in the wok, while dry, days-old grains stay separate and sear.
4. Dice the carrots small, about pea-sized so they cook through in a minute, and box them. That's the mise — mise en place, cook-speak for everything cut and ready — and it means eating night is all cooking, no chopping.

## Weeknight

- Set up before any heat, because the wok waits for no one: break the cold rice into loose grains with wet hands, beat the eggs in a bowl with a pinch of salt, slice the green onions thin, and line up carrots, minced garlic and ginger, frozen peas straight from the bag, sliced pork, soy sauce, sesame oil, and white pepper within arm's reach of the stove.
- Heat the wok on the highest burner until a drop of water skitters and vanishes, then add a glug of neutral oil and watch it: first it shimmers, then the first wisp of smoke rises. That wisp is the signal — the wok is properly hot, and everything from here cooks in seconds.
- Half batches, always — half the rice and half of everything else per round. A crowded wok loses its heat and steams instead of sears.
- Batch one: pour in half the eggs and scramble softly, 20 to 30 seconds, out onto a plate while still glossy. A little more oil, then half the carrots for 60 seconds, and half the garlic and ginger for 15 seconds, just until fragrant. Half the rice in: press it flat against the metal with the spatula, let it sear untouched 20 to 30 seconds, then toss and press again, about 2 minutes total, until the grains separate and start to pop and hop against the wok.
- Still batch one: add half the peas, half the pork, and the eggs back in and toss. Pour soy sauce around the rim of the wok, not onto the rice — running down the screaming-hot metal it toasts and caramelizes on the way in, a flavor plain poured soy never gets. Finish with a small splash of sesame oil, a pinch of white pepper, and half the green onions off the heat. Tip it into a serving bowl and cover with foil.
- Batch two: wipe the wok if anything stuck, then repeat the whole sequence. Batch one waits under the foil, and no one has ever noticed.
- Extra char siu on top of each bowl, because you roasted extra and you know it.

## Notes

- Batch one waits under foil for batch two — a crowded wok steams, and steamed fried rice is just Tuesday rice.
- Kendra version: extra peas and carrots in her batch, lighter rice. The wok does not judge.
- Day 5 check: the rice is better day 3 than day 1, and the glazed pork holds all week. The fried rice itself is a cook-tonight, eat-tonight event.
